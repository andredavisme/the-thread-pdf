Business Context

The First Commitment Record

The quote has been accepted. The customer has said yes. At this moment, the nature of the data changes. Everything up to this point — the request, the estimate, the quote — was prospective. It described what might happen. The order is the first record that describes what will happen. It is a commitment: your organization has agreed to deliver something specific, at a specific price, by a specific date.

The Tier I order table captured the skeleton of that commitment: a link to the accepted quote, a confirmed cost and price, and an expected delivery date. That is enough to know an order exists. It is not enough to manage one. Consider an order for 200 units of three different products, split across two delivery locations, with one product back-ordered. A single record with a single delivery date and a single price cannot represent that reality. The moment operational complexity enters — and it always does — the header record alone collapses.

One field anchors the entire order in time: ord_date — the date the order was confirmed. System-generated at entry, never changed. It is the reference point for every aging, cycle time, and pipeline age calculation the order will ever participate in.

The Data Gap

What You Can't Answer Yet

  • What specific products or services are included in this order, and in what quantities?
  • If an order ships in two deliveries, which line items were in each shipment?
  • What delivery date did the customer request, versus what date was operationally promised?
  • Which line items are back-ordered or delayed, and by how long?
  • What is the fulfillment status of each line item independently?
  • How does order composition compare to quote composition — were any items changed at order entry?

Without line-level detail, you cannot route, pick, schedule, or track individual items. Without scheduling fields, you cannot distinguish a customer-requested date from an internal operational target. Without partial fulfillment support, a single delayed line item holds the entire order hostage in your reporting.

Core Concept

Order Lines: Commitment + Fulfillment State

The order_line table mirrors the structure of estimate_line and quote_line — one row per SKU per order. But it carries something neither predecessor does: fulfillment state. An estimate line has a cost. A quote line has a customer-facing price. An order line has a quantity committed, a quantity fulfilled, and a status tracking the gap between them.

Tier II fields are set at order entry — they describe what was ordered. Tier III fields are updated during fulfillment — they describe what happened. This separation makes it possible to query fulfillment state at any point without confusing the original commitment with the operational outcome.

Core Concept

Scheduling: Three Dates, Three Meanings

One of the most common sources of confusion in order management data is the conflation of multiple dates into a single field. “Delivery date” sounds like one thing. In practice, it is at least three — and conflating them makes every schedule-variance query unreliable.

DateMeaningWhere It Lives
Customer-requestedWhen the customer wants deliveryrequest table — already captured at intake via req_id
Promised dateWhat your org committed to in the quoteord_promised_date — new at order stage
Operational targetInternal working date based on actual capacityord_scheduled_date — set by operations

The customer-requested date is not a new piece of information — it was captured in the request table. The order already carries req_id as a foreign key, so the requested date is retrieved by joining, not by duplicating. The two dates that are new at the order stage are the promised date and the scheduled date, because they did not exist until the quote was accepted and operations engaged.

Note: ord_exp_date from Tier I is superseded by these two fields in Tier II. The three-date view is assembled by joining all three source tables in a single query.

Core Concept

Partial Fulfillment

Not every order ships in a single delivery. Partial fulfillment is managed at two levels. At the line level: ord_ln_qty_fulfilled tracks cumulative quantity delivered per line; ord_ln_status reflects whether a line is open, partially fulfilled, or complete — a line is not Fulfilled until qty_fulfilled equals qty_ordered. At the order level: the order header reflects the aggregate state of its lines — Open if no lines shipped, Partial if some are fulfilled, Fulfilled only when every line is complete.

Core Concept

Schema vs. Database Functions

The schema defines what data exists and how it is structured. It does not define how that data is maintained. Fields marked as “updated as deliveries occur” or “calculated” are candidates for automation in the database layer — triggers that execute automatically in response to data events, and computed fields calculated on write rather than stored manually.

A trigger watching order_line can update the parent order automatically: when the first line changes to Partial or Fulfilled, set ord_status = 'Partial'; when all lines equal Fulfilled, set ord_status = 'Fulfilled' and stamp ord_fulfilled_date. Similarly, ord_ln_total — defined as qty × unit price — is a computed field that guarantees accuracy without depending on human consistency.

This textbook defines the schema. The database implementation — triggers, functions, constraints, computed columns — is built in Phase II. The schema is the specification; the database is the enforcement.

Analytical Application

Queries That Become Possible

Open orders with line-level fulfillment status — the daily operations view

SELECT o.ord_id, c.cust_name, o.ord_promised_date, o.ord_scheduled_date,
  o.ord_status,
  COUNT(ol.ord_ln_id) AS total_lines,
  COUNT(CASE WHEN ol.ord_ln_status = 'Fulfilled' THEN 1 END) AS fulfilled_lines,
  COUNT(CASE WHEN ol.ord_ln_status = 'Back-Ordered' THEN 1 END) AS backordered_lines
FROM "order" o
JOIN customer c ON o.cust_id = c.cust_id
JOIN order_line ol ON o.ord_id = ol.ord_id
WHERE o.ord_status IN ('Open', 'Partial')
GROUP BY o.ord_id, c.cust_name, o.ord_promised_date, o.ord_scheduled_date, o.ord_status
ORDER BY o.ord_promised_date ASC;

Three-date view: request, promise, schedule

SELECT o.ord_id, c.cust_name,
  r.req_delivery_date AS customer_requested_date,
  o.ord_promised_date,
  o.ord_scheduled_date,
  o.ord_promised_date - r.req_delivery_date AS promise_gap_days,
  o.ord_scheduled_date - o.ord_promised_date AS schedule_slip_days
FROM "order" o
JOIN customer c ON o.cust_id = c.cust_id
JOIN request r ON o.req_id = r.req_id
WHERE r.req_delivery_date IS NOT NULL
  AND o.ord_promised_date IS NOT NULL
  AND o.ord_scheduled_date IS NOT NULL
ORDER BY promise_gap_days DESC;

Demonstrates how the FK chain (ord_id → req_id → request) makes the requested date accessible without storing it redundantly on the order.

Partial fulfillment rate by SKU

SELECT s.sku_code, s.sku_name,
  COUNT(ol.ord_ln_id) AS total_order_lines,
  COUNT(CASE WHEN ol.ord_ln_status = 'Back-Ordered' THEN 1 END) AS backordered_lines,
  ROUND(
    COUNT(CASE WHEN ol.ord_ln_status IN ('Partial','Back-Ordered') THEN 1 END)::decimal
    / NULLIF(COUNT(ol.ord_ln_id), 0) * 100, 1
  ) AS incomplete_rate_pct
FROM order_line ol
JOIN sku s ON ol.sku_id = s.sku_id
GROUP BY s.sku_id, s.sku_code, s.sku_name
ORDER BY incomplete_rate_pct DESC;

Order value variance: quoted price vs. confirmed order price

SELECT o.ord_id, c.cust_name,
  q.qte_price AS quoted_price, o.ord_price AS order_price,
  o.ord_price - q.qte_price AS price_delta,
  ROUND((o.ord_price - q.qte_price) / NULLIF(q.qte_price, 0) * 100, 2) AS delta_pct
FROM "order" o
JOIN quote q ON o.qte_id = q.qte_id
JOIN customer c ON o.cust_id = c.cust_id
WHERE o.ord_price <> q.qte_price
ORDER BY ABS(o.ord_price - q.qte_price) DESC;

A negative delta means a discount was applied at order entry without a quote revision — a margin leak that bypassed the approval workflow.

Reflection

Questions Worth Sitting With

  • Think about the most complex order your organization has fulfilled in the past year. How many distinct line items did it contain? How many deliveries did it take? Could your current data model reconstruct a complete picture of that order's fulfillment journey?
  • The customer's requested delivery date lives in the request — not the order. Does your current system preserve the original customer ask after the order is confirmed? If not, what is lost when that date is overwritten by the promised or scheduled date?
  • Consider a product that has been back-ordered more than once in the past six months. Is that pattern visible in your data? What would it take to surface it with a single query?
  • If an order ships in three partial deliveries over three weeks, how does your current system reflect the order's status at each stage? At what point does the order look “closed” in your data — and does that match when the customer considers it complete?
  • Think about the fields in this chapter described as “updated as deliveries occur” or “calculated.” In your current environment, who is responsible for keeping those values current? How often are they wrong — and what would it take to make their maintenance automatic?
