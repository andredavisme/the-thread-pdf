Chapter 6

Delivering on the Promise

Tier III  ·  Delivery tracking, actual vs. promised dates, carrier data, partial shipments

Business Context

The First Backward-Looking Record

The order described what your organization committed to. The delivery describes what actually happened. Every date comparison discussed in previous chapters — requested vs. promised, promised vs. scheduled — has been forward-looking. The delivery record is the first backward-looking entry in the journey: it captures reality, not intention.

It is also the first record your customer experiences directly. They do not see your estimate, your internal margin calculations, or your scheduling variance. They see whether the product arrived, in the right quantity, on the date they needed it. That asymmetry — between what you measured internally and what the customer experienced externally — is what makes the delivery table analytically powerful and operationally critical.

The single most valuable calculation in delivery analytics is also the simplest: del_date − ord_promised_date. Positive means late. Negative means early. Zero means on time. But this calculation only works if both dates are in the data and linked to the same record. The foreign key ord_id on the delivery record is what connects them — transforming every delivery record from a log entry into a performance measurement.

The Data Gap

What You Can't Answer with Tier I

  • Was this delivery on time relative to the promised date — or the customer's original request?
  • Did the delivery fulfill the entire order, or only part of it?
  • Which specific order lines were included in this shipment?
  • Who carried it, and by what method?
  • If there were multiple deliveries against one order, which one completed it?
  • Was anything delivered that was not on the order?

A complete delivery record captures four things: when (actual delivery date), what (which order lines, in what quantities), how (carrier, method, tracking reference), and against what commitment (the promised date being measured against). The Tier I record had only the first. Tier III adds the rest.

Core Concept

del_type: Knowing Whether the Order Is Done

The most operationally important field in this table answers the question every operations and customer service team asks when a shipment goes out: does this close the order?

Full — this single delivery fulfills the entire order
Partial — this delivery fulfills some lines but more shipments are expected
Final-Partial — this is the last shipment; it may not have fulfilled every line completely (back-orders cancelled, substitutions accepted), but the order is considered closed

Final-Partial is the structurally important edge case. It closes the order even when fulfillment was incomplete — capturing the real-world reality that not every order ends with 100% of every line shipped.
Core Concept

New Table: delivery_line

For full delivery tracking — particularly in multi-shipment orders — a delivery_line table records which specific order lines were included in each shipment, and in what quantity. This is the Tier III extension of the line-item pattern established in Chapters 3, 4, and 5.

ColumnAttributeNotes
del_ln_idDelivery Line IDIII Primary key
del_idDelivery IDIII FK → delivery.del_id

When a delivery_line record is created, it triggers an update to the corresponding order_line: ord_ln_qty_fulfilled increases by del_ln_qty, and ord_ln_status is recalculated. When all order lines reach Fulfilled — or a Final-Partial delivery is recorded — the parent order transitions to Fulfilled and ord_fulfilled_date is stamped. This is the trigger chain described in Chapter 5, now shown in its operational context.

Core Concept

Multi-Shipment Orders

Partial delivery is not a failure state — it is a normal operational pattern. Each delivery against an order creates a new delivery header and corresponding delivery_line rows. The order's fulfillment state is the aggregate of all delivery lines against all order lines.

Example — order with three lines (50 + 20 + 10 units):

First delivery: Lines 1 and 2 ship in full. del_type = Partial. Order status: Partial.
Second delivery: Line 3 ships. del_type = Final-Partial (or Full). Order status: Fulfilled. ord_fulfilled_date stamped.

Without delivery_line, the second delivery looks identical to the first — a timestamp against an order ID. With delivery_line, you can reconstruct the complete fulfillment sequence, line by line, shipment by shipment.
Connects Forward

The Boundary Between Internal and External

The delivery table sits at the boundary between your internal operations and your customer's reality. Every date comparison it enables — actual vs. promised, actual vs. requested — is a measure of the gap between your commitment and the customer's experience.

  • Chapter 7 — Adjustment: When a delivery falls short, the adjustment is the mechanism for correcting the record and the relationship. The delivery record is the evidence the adjustment references.
  • Chapter 8 — Response: The response record captures how the customer reacted. The delivery record captures what they reacted to. Together, they form the complete feedback loop.

del_carrier and del_date, consistently captured, make carrier performance analysis possible — surfacing which carriers are consistently late and where logistics contracts may need renegotiation.

Analytical Application

Queries That Become Possible

On-time delivery rate by month

SELECT
  DATE_TRUNC('month', d.del_date) AS delivery_month,
  COUNT(d.del_id) AS total_deliveries,
  COUNT(CASE WHEN d.del_date <= o.ord_promised_date THEN 1 END) AS on_time,
  ROUND(
    COUNT(CASE WHEN d.del_date <= o.ord_promised_date THEN 1 END)::decimal
    / NULLIF(COUNT(d.del_id), 0) * 100, 1
  ) AS on_time_pct
FROM delivery d
JOIN "order" o ON d.ord_id = o.ord_id
WHERE o.ord_promised_date IS NOT NULL
GROUP BY DATE_TRUNC('month', d.del_date)
ORDER BY delivery_month ASC;

On-time delivery rate by carrier

SELECT d.del_carrier, d.del_method,
  COUNT(d.del_id) AS total_deliveries,
  COUNT(CASE WHEN d.del_date <= o.ord_promised_date THEN 1 END) AS on_time,
  ROUND(
    COUNT(CASE WHEN d.del_date <= o.ord_promised_date THEN 1 END)::decimal
    / NULLIF(COUNT(d.del_id), 0) * 100, 1
  ) AS on_time_pct,
  ROUND(AVG(d.del_date - o.ord_promised_date), 1) AS avg_days_variance
FROM delivery d
JOIN "order" o ON d.ord_id = o.ord_id
WHERE d.del_carrier IS NOT NULL AND o.ord_promised_date IS NOT NULL
GROUP BY d.del_carrier, d.del_method
ORDER BY on_time_pct DESC;

avg_days_variance is the richer signal — 85% on-time with +12 days average variance is more damaging than 90% on-time with +4 days.

Full delivery journey: requested → promised → actual

SELECT o.ord_id, c.cust_name,
  r.req_delivery_date AS customer_requested,
  o.ord_promised_date AS promised,
  d.del_date AS actual,
  o.ord_promised_date - r.req_delivery_date AS promise_gap_days,
  d.del_date - o.ord_promised_date AS delivery_variance_days,
  d.del_date - r.req_delivery_date AS total_gap_from_request
FROM delivery d
JOIN "order" o ON d.ord_id = o.ord_id
JOIN request r ON o.req_id = r.req_id
JOIN customer c ON o.cust_id = c.cust_id
WHERE d.del_type IN ('Full', 'Final-Partial')
ORDER BY total_gap_from_request DESC;

Filters to Full and Final-Partial ensures one row per closed order. total_gap_from_request is the number that matters most to the customer.

Multi-shipment orders: delivery count and completion timeline

SELECT o.ord_id, c.cust_name,
  COUNT(d.del_id) AS delivery_count,
  MIN(d.del_date) AS first_delivery,
  MAX(d.del_date) AS final_delivery,
  MAX(d.del_date) - MIN(d.del_date) AS fulfillment_span_days,
  o.ord_promised_date,
  MAX(d.del_date) - o.ord_promised_date AS final_delivery_variance
FROM delivery d
JOIN "order" o ON d.ord_id = o.ord_id
JOIN customer c ON o.cust_id = c.cust_id
GROUP BY o.ord_id, c.cust_name, o.ord_promised_date
HAVING COUNT(d.del_id) > 1
ORDER BY delivery_count DESC, fulfillment_span_days DESC;

Line-level fulfillment completeness

SELECT ol.ord_ln_id, o.ord_id, c.cust_name, s.sku_code, s.sku_name,
  ol.ord_ln_qty_ordered,
  COALESCE(SUM(dl.del_ln_qty), 0) AS qty_delivered,
  ol.ord_ln_qty_ordered - COALESCE(SUM(dl.del_ln_qty), 0) AS qty_shortfall,
  ol.ord_ln_status
FROM order_line ol
JOIN "order" o ON ol.ord_id = o.ord_id
JOIN customer c ON o.cust_id = c.cust_id
JOIN sku s ON ol.sku_id = s.sku_id
LEFT JOIN delivery_line dl ON ol.ord_ln_id = dl.ord_ln_id
GROUP BY ol.ord_ln_id, o.ord_id, c.cust_name, s.sku_code, s.sku_name, ol.ord_ln_qty_ordered, ol.ord_ln_status
HAVING ol.ord_ln_qty_ordered > COALESCE(SUM(dl.del_ln_qty), 0)
ORDER BY qty_shortfall DESC;

The shortfall column feeds directly into the adjustment analysis in Chapter 7.

Reflection

Questions Worth Sitting With

  • Think about the last delivery complaint you received from a customer. Was the issue a late delivery, a partial delivery, or something else? Is that distinction visible in your current data?
  • Your organization likely uses more than one carrier. Do you currently have a way to compare on-time performance across carriers by method? What decisions are being made about logistics contracts without that data?
  • Consider an order that required three deliveries to complete. In your current system, is it possible to reconstruct the sequence — which lines shipped when, in what quantities, and which delivery closed the order?
  • The del_type field distinguishes Full, Partial, and Final-Partial. Think about a recent order that closed without everything being delivered. How was that closure recorded? Where did the shortfall go?
  • Think about a recent case where a delivery triggered a correction. What data would you need from the delivery record to support that adjustment clearly?
