Preface

This textbook is designed for practitioners — the people who work inside sales operations, customer success, order management, and business intelligence teams. It does not assume you are a data scientist. It assumes you work with customers, handle transactions, and at some point have asked: "Why can’t I just get a straight answer from our data?"

The answer to that question usually lies not in the tools, but in the foundation. Data that lacks structure, discipline, and a clear model of the customer journey will frustrate every analyst, every dashboard, and every business review it touches. This textbook builds that foundation — from a single customer record to a fully operational sales intelligence system.

Introduction

The Customer’s Data Journey

When managing information about the services and products you offer, a strong understanding of the customer’s journey from a data perspective is essential. Debris comes in the form of redundant information, personal bias, and reckless KPI hunts. Communication gets lost. Teams build shadow spreadsheets. Reports contradict each other. All of this stems from one root cause: the data model was never designed around the actual journey the customer takes.

Core Concept

The Universal Customer Journey

Across industries and organizations, the most common supporting data follows the same logical progression — not a sales funnel, but a data lineage. Each step creates a record that references the step before it.

  • Customer — Who is asking?
  • Customer Request — What are they asking for?
  • Estimate — What will it take to fulfill the request?
  • Quote — What is the formal offer?
  • Order — What has been agreed upon?
  • Delivery — What was actually delivered, and when?
  • Adjustments — What changed along the way?
  • Customer Response — How did the customer react?
This chain of references is what allows you to trace any outcome back to its origin: a delivery dispute back to the original quote, a profitability variance back to the estimate, a customer churn signal back to unresolved adjustments.
Foundation

Six Baseline Questions

The Tier I schema is intentionally minimal — a clean, unambiguous foundation before complexity is added. It answers six baseline questions that remain the health check throughout the entire textbook:

  • How many open orders exist?
  • How many customers do we have?
  • What is our delivery rate?
  • How is our customer satisfaction?
  • Who are our most profitable customers?
  • Who are our new customers?

A Note on Design

Foreign Key Redundancy — Intentional

Several tables carry the full chain of foreign keys — cust_id, req_id, est_id, etc. — even when they are technically derivable by joining through parent tables. This is an intentional Tier I design choice for two reasons:

  1. Query simplicity — analysts can join directly to any record without traversing multiple tables
  2. Audit transparency — every record knows its complete lineage without requiring a join

As the schema matures in later tiers, some redundancy is deliberately normalized out. Chapter 4 renames est_id on the quote table to qte_est_id to align naming conventions. Chapter 5 replaces ord_exp_date with two purpose-specific date fields. Each change is documented with a supersession note in the relevant chapter. For now, clarity wins over purity.

How to Use This Textbook

Chapter Structure

Each chapter follows a consistent five-part structure:

  1. Business Context — The real-world situation motivating the new data need
  2. The Data Gap — What the current schema cannot answer
  3. Schema Update — New columns or tables, with rationale for every addition
  4. Analytical Application — SQL queries, example reports, or KPI definitions
  5. Reflection — Questions to apply the concept in your own organization
The schema workbook (Customer_Processing_Schema.xlsx) is updated alongside each chapter. Each worksheet maps to a table, and the Tier column tracks when each attribute was introduced. You do not need to build everything at once — the tier system is designed for incremental implementation.
