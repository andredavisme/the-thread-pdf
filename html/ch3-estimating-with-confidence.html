Estimating with Confidence

Business Context

Where Ambition Meets Reality

An estimate is your organization's internal answer to a customer's request. It is the moment where you translate "can you do this?" into "here is what it will take, here is what it will cost us, and here is what we will charge."

Tier I captures the skeleton of that answer: an estimated delivery date, a cost, a price, and a field for alerts. That is enough to know an estimate existed. It is not enough to understand how it was built, what it is made of, or how confidently it was made.

Consider a job that comes in over budget at delivery. You pull up the estimate and find a single cost figure: $14,200. Was that one line item or forty? Was it a product cost, a labor cost, or both? Did anyone flag a risk before the job started? The data cannot tell you. The estimate was recorded as an outcome, not as a process — and when something goes wrong, there is nothing to audit.

Tier II changes that. It introduces the building blocks of the estimate: individual products and services being priced, the margin being targeted, structured risk flagging, and — for the first time in this schema — the person responsible for the work.

The Data Gap

What You Can't Answer Yet

  • What specific products or services are included in this estimate?
  • What is the margin on this estimate, and is it within acceptable bounds?
  • How was the estimated cost derived — catalog price, custom calculation, or a guess?
  • Were there risks or constraints flagged during estimation, and what were they?
  • How does this estimate compare to similar ones for similar requests?
  • Who prepared this estimate, and when?

The Tier I est_alerts field acknowledges that alerts exist, but stores them as free text. A flag buried in a text field is not actionable data — it is a note. Tier II makes alerts structured, trackable, and queryable.

Core Concept

Cost vs. Price: The Margin Gap

The Tier I estimate table already carries both est_cost and est_price. But the presence of both fields creates an obligation: use them correctly and consistently, or the margin data they produce is worthless.

Cost is what it takes to fulfill the request — materials, labor, overhead, logistics. Price is what the customer pays: cost plus your margin, adjusted for market conditions, customer tier, and strategy. The gap between them is margin — and margin is one of the most important numbers in any sales operation.

Estimated Margin % = (est_price − est_cost) / est_price × 100

This requires no new fields — it is derived from what Tier I already provides. At the estimate stage, margin is still a target. It has not been committed to a quote or tested against a customer response. That makes the estimate the earliest and most valuable place to catch structurally unprofitable deals before they ever leave the building.

Core Concept

Introducing SKUs

A SKU — Stock Keeping Unit — is the atomic unit of what you sell. Without SKUs, an estimate for "installation services" is a description. With a SKU, it is a reference to a defined service item with a standard cost, a standard price, a lead time, and a history of how it has been estimated and delivered.

SKUs belong in their own table — a catalog of everything you offer. The estimate references a SKU by ID rather than copying its details. This is the principle of normalization: store information once, reference it everywhere. A single estimate can cover multiple products or services via estimate lines — one row per SKU, per estimate.

Core Concept

Estimate Alerts: From Text to Structure

Tier II replaces the free-text est_alerts field with two structured fields: est_alert_type (a controlled vocabulary — e.g., Supply Risk, Margin Below Threshold, Lead Time Uncertainty, Requires Approval) and est_alert_notes (free-text detail for human review).

This separation — type for filtering and routing, notes for context — is a pattern that will appear throughout this textbook. Structured fields answer questions. Free-text fields provide explanations. Both are needed; neither can do the other's job.

Core Concept

Individual Accountability and the User Table

Estimates are prepared by people. The naive approach is a text field: est_prepared_by = 'Maria Santos' — until she appears as "M. Santos" in one record and "msantos" in another. Text fields applied to people decay into inconsistency. The right approach: store a reference to a user table.

The user table serves three purposes beyond the estimate: (1) Role consolidation — a user's role is stored once and referenced everywhere; (2) Consistent ownership — req_owner from Ch2 and every future assignment field all reference this same table, enabling cross-table workload queries; (3) Future security — in Phase II, this table anchors row-level security and audit logging.

Analytical Application

Queries That Become Possible

Estimated margin on each open estimate

SELECT e.est_id, c.cust_name, e.est_cost, e.est_price,
  ROUND((e.est_price - e.est_cost) / NULLIF(e.est_price, 0) * 100, 1) AS margin_pct
FROM estimate e
JOIN customer c ON e.cust_id = c.cust_id
JOIN request r ON e.req_id = r.req_id
WHERE r.req_status NOT IN ('Closed — Fulfilled', 'Closed — Declined')
ORDER BY margin_pct ASC;

Sorting ascending surfaces the most at-risk deals first — the ones most likely to be unprofitable if they convert.

Estimates with active alerts requiring approval

SELECT e.est_id, c.cust_name,
  u.user_first_name || ' ' || u.user_last_name AS prepared_by,
  e.est_alert_type, e.est_alert_notes, e.est_date
FROM estimate e
JOIN customer c ON e.cust_id = c.cust_id
JOIN "user" u ON e.est_prepared_by = u.user_id
WHERE e.est_alert_type = 'Requires Approval'
ORDER BY e.est_date ASC;

Estimated cost vs. standard cost variance by SKU

SELECT s.sku_code, s.sku_name,
  ROUND(AVG(el.est_line_cost), 2) AS avg_estimated_cost,
  s.sku_std_cost AS standard_cost,
  ROUND(AVG(el.est_line_cost) - s.sku_std_cost, 2) AS avg_cost_variance
FROM estimate_line el
JOIN sku s ON el.sku_id = s.sku_id
GROUP BY s.sku_id, s.sku_code, s.sku_name, s.sku_std_cost
ORDER BY avg_cost_variance DESC;

A persistent positive variance may mean standard cost is outdated. Negative variance may mean unapproved discounting.

Most accurate estimators (estimate vs. order actuals)

SELECT u.user_first_name || ' ' || u.user_last_name AS estimator,
  COUNT(e.est_id) AS estimates_prepared,
  ROUND(AVG(ABS(e.est_cost - o.ord_cost)), 2) AS avg_cost_variance,
  ROUND(AVG(ABS(e.est_price - o.ord_price)), 2) AS avg_price_variance
FROM estimate e
JOIN "user" u ON e.est_prepared_by = u.user_id
JOIN "order" o ON e.est_id = o.est_id
GROUP BY u.user_id, estimator
ORDER BY avg_cost_variance ASC;

Users with both open requests and pending estimates

SELECT u.user_id, u.user_first_name || ' ' || u.user_last_name AS user_name,
  COUNT(DISTINCT r.req_id) AS open_requests,
  COUNT(DISTINCT e.est_id) AS pending_estimates
FROM "user" u
LEFT JOIN request r ON r.req_owner = u.user_id AND r.req_status = 'Open'
LEFT JOIN estimate e ON e.est_prepared_by = u.user_id AND e.est_alert_type IS NOT NULL
GROUP BY u.user_id, user_name
HAVING COUNT(DISTINCT r.req_id) > 0 AND COUNT(DISTINCT e.est_id) > 0
ORDER BY open_requests DESC;

This cross-table query is only possible because both req_owner and est_prepared_by reference the same user table.

Reflection

Questions Worth Sitting With

  • When your organization prepares an estimate today, is it a single number or a collection of line items? If it is a single number, what information is lost when that estimate is reviewed months later?
  • Think about your most common products or services. Could you assign each one a SKU with a standard cost and a standard price? What would prevent that standardization, and what would it take to get there?
  • How are people currently identified in your data systems? Are names stored as text in multiple places? What inconsistencies has that created, and what would a single user record resolve?
  • If you could query every open request and every pending estimate by the person responsible for each — in a single query — what would that tell you about your team's current workload distribution?
  • Has your organization ever delivered a job that was profitable on paper but a loss in practice? What information, captured at the estimate stage, might have predicted that outcome?
