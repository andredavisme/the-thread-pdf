Business Context

The First Artifact the Customer Sees

The estimate was an internal document. The quote is the first artifact the customer sees — and that distinction changes everything. An estimate is typically the result of an internal conversation: a working analysis of cost, feasibility, and timing. A quote is different. In the real world, a quote is almost certainly a shared document: a PDF, a formal letter, an email with attached pricing, or a portal-generated summary. It carries your organization's name, a price, a delivery commitment, and an implied promise. Once sent, it creates expectations. Once accepted, it becomes the contractual foundation of the order.

Core Concept

Data and Artifacts: The Distinction That Matters

Data is the structured record your organization owns, secures, and controls — it lives in your database, is referenced by other records, and governed by your access policies. An artifact is the presentation of that data, formatted for a specific audience. A quote document sent to a customer is an artifact: derived from the data, but not the data itself.

The data outlives the artifact. A quote PDF may be deleted, lost, or never saved. The data record persists, traceable and auditable. This is why the schema must capture everything of operational value — not assume the document will always be available.

The artifact is shaped for its audience. A quote document shows the customer price and delivery date. It does not show your internal cost, your margin targets, or your alert flags. The same underlying data produces different artifacts for different audiences.

Data is owned; artifacts are shared. The estimate existed entirely in the internal sphere. The quote crosses the boundary — and from this point forward, every record has a potential external representation.

Core Concept

Quote Versioning

In practice, quotes are rarely accepted on the first draft. Pricing gets negotiated. Delivery dates shift. Line items get added or removed. In a Tier I schema where each quote is a single mutable record, every revision destroys the history of what came before it.

The solution is a self-referencing parent key — the same pattern introduced for account hierarchy in Chapter 1. A new column qte_parent_id references qte_id in the same table. The original quote has no parent (NULL). Each revision points back to the quote it superseded. A companion integer qte_version makes the sequence explicit: version 1 is the original, incrementing per revision. The active quote is always the highest version number within a qte_parent_id group.

When a deal closes, you can now trace the full revision history — how many versions it took, what changed at each step, and whether the final price was above or below the initial offer. This is the negotiation audit trail that every sales manager wants but almost no one has captured cleanly.

Core Concept

Revisions vs. Adjustments: The Right Boundary

Quote versioning handles changes made before the quote is accepted — negotiation-stage revisions where no order exists yet. The adjustment table handles a fundamentally different kind of change: corrections made after agreement has been reached — against a committed order or completed delivery.

MechanismTimingTriggerRecorded In
qte_parent_id / qte_versionPre-acceptanceNegotiation, scope change, pricing updatequote table — new row per version
adj_*Post-acceptancePrice correction, date change, quantity change, creditadjustment table — linked to ord_id or del_id
💡 Key Concept

A practical test: "Has the customer said yes yet?" If no — use a quote revision. If yes — use an adjustment. This separation keeps each table clean and purposeful: the quote table tells the story of how an agreement was reached; the adjustment table tells the story of how reality diverged from it after the fact.

Core Concept

Approval Workflows

Not every quote should leave your organization without a second set of eyes. Three fields capture the complete approval record: qte_prepared_by (FK → user), qte_approved_by (FK → user; NULL if not yet approved), and qte_approved_date. A quote with an approver but no date is a data quality flag — approval happened verbally without documentation. Once consistently populated, you can compare conversion rates by approver: if one manager's quotes convert at 60% and another's at 35%, that is a pricing strategy signal worth investigating.

Core Concept

Quote Expiry

Quotes have a shelf life. Costs change, capacity fills, supplier pricing fluctuates. Two fields address this: qte_valid_until (the operational anchor — a daily query on this date produces the action list) and qte_expiry_action (a controlled vocabulary: Renewed, Cancelled, Expired-No-Action, Superseded). A structured expiry action makes the question "how many quotes expired with no follow-up in Q2?" answerable with a single WHERE clause.

Core Concept

Tracking the Conversation

qte_date is the system-generated creation timestamp — the reference for every pipeline age calculation and expiry measurement. qte_sent_date records when the quote was delivered to the customer. qte_response_date records when they replied. The gap between sent and response is the customer's decision window — patterns in that window, by customer type or quote value, directly inform follow-up cadence and pipeline forecasting.

Analytical Application

Queries That Become Possible

Expired quotes without a closed status — the daily operations query

SELECT q.qte_id, q.qte_version, c.cust_name, q.qte_price,
  q.qte_valid_until,
  u.user_first_name || ' ' || u.user_last_name AS prepared_by
FROM quote q
JOIN customer c ON q.cust_id = c.cust_id
JOIN "user" u ON q.qte_prepared_by = u.user_id
WHERE q.qte_valid_until < CURRENT_DATE
  AND q.qte_expiry_action IS NULL
ORDER BY q.qte_valid_until ASC;

Each row is a follow-up action — renew, cancel, or log the outcome.

Average revisions before acceptance, by customer type

SELECT c.cust_type,
  AVG(rc.version_count) AS avg_revisions_to_acceptance
FROM (
  SELECT q.cust_id,
    COALESCE(q.qte_parent_id, q.qte_id) AS quote_group,
    COUNT(q.qte_id) AS version_count
  FROM quote q
  WHERE q.qte_id IN (SELECT qte_id FROM "order")
  GROUP BY q.cust_id, COALESCE(q.qte_parent_id, q.qte_id)
) rc
JOIN customer c ON rc.cust_id = c.cust_id
GROUP BY c.cust_type ORDER BY avg_revisions_to_acceptance DESC;

Quoted vs. estimated price delta by customer tier

SELECT c.cust_type, COUNT(*) AS quote_count,
  ROUND(AVG(q.qte_price - e.est_price), 2) AS avg_price_delta,
  ROUND(AVG((q.qte_price - e.est_price) / NULLIF(e.est_price,0) * 100), 2) AS avg_pct_delta
FROM quote q
JOIN estimate e ON q.qte_est_id = e.est_id
JOIN customer c ON q.cust_id = c.cust_id
WHERE q.qte_version = 1
GROUP BY c.cust_type ORDER BY avg_pct_delta ASC;

A negative avg_price_delta means quotes are going out below estimate — margin is being given away at the quote stage.

Conversion rate by approver

SELECT u.user_first_name || ' ' || u.user_last_name AS approver,
  COUNT(q.qte_id) AS quotes_approved,
  COUNT(o.ord_id) AS quotes_converted,
  ROUND(COUNT(o.ord_id)::decimal / NULLIF(COUNT(q.qte_id),0) * 100, 1) AS conversion_rate_pct
FROM quote q
JOIN "user" u ON q.qte_approved_by = u.user_id
LEFT JOIN "order" o ON q.qte_id = o.qte_id
WHERE q.qte_approved_by IS NOT NULL
GROUP BY u.user_id, u.user_first_name, u.user_last_name
ORDER BY conversion_rate_pct DESC;

Days from quote sent to customer response, by customer tier

SELECT c.cust_type,
  COUNT(*) AS responded_quotes,
  ROUND(AVG(q.qte_response_date - q.qte_sent_date), 1) AS avg_days_to_response,
  MIN(q.qte_response_date - q.qte_sent_date) AS fastest_response,
  MAX(q.qte_response_date - q.qte_sent_date) AS slowest_response
FROM quote q
JOIN customer c ON q.cust_id = c.cust_id
WHERE q.qte_sent_date IS NOT NULL
  AND q.qte_response_date IS NOT NULL
GROUP BY c.cust_type ORDER BY avg_days_to_response ASC;

If Reseller accounts respond in 3 days but Direct accounts take 12, the follow-up playbook should reflect that difference.

Reflection

Questions Worth Sitting With

  • In your current process, how many versions does a typical quote go through before it is accepted or rejected? Is that number tracked anywhere today?
  • Think about the last time a quote went out and the customer came back with a counteroffer. What changed? Was that change captured anywhere in your data — or did it live only in an email thread?
  • Who in your organization has the authority to approve a quote? Is there a formal threshold — price, margin, delivery timeline — that triggers a review, or is the process informal?
  • Consider the difference between the quote document your customer receives and the quote record in your system. What information does the document contain that the record does not — and vice versa?
  • How many quotes in your current pipeline have passed their stated validity date without a recorded outcome? What would it mean for your pipeline accuracy if you could answer that with a single query?
  • Think about a change that happened after a customer accepted a quote — a price correction, a date push, a quantity change. Now that you understand the boundary between revisions and adjustments, where would that change belong in this schema?
