Business Context

The Record That Closes the Loop

Every chapter in this book has described data your organization creates: the request you logged, the estimate you built, the quote you sent, the order you confirmed, the delivery you made, the adjustment you applied. The response record is different. It captures what the customer experienced — their words, their rating, their signal of whether they intend to return.

The response is structurally the last node in the journey, but operationally it is the beginning of the next one. A satisfied customer becomes a new request. A dissatisfied customer who was followed up with and resolved becomes a recoverable relationship. A dissatisfied customer who was never contacted becomes a lost account — often without you knowing why. The response record determines which of those three outcomes is visible and which is invisible.

Most organizations collect some form of customer feedback. Very few connect it back to the specific delivery, order, or adjustment that drove it. Without that connection, feedback is a feeling. With it, feedback is evidence — and evidence can be acted on, trended, and used to hold operational decisions accountable.

The Data Gap

What You Can't Answer with Tier I

  • What was the customer's satisfaction score for this specific delivery?
  • Was a follow-up conducted after a negative response — and did it resolve the issue?
  • What is the NPS score by customer segment, product category, or delivery performance band?
  • Which customers have not responded at all — and how long have they been silent?
  • Did the adjustment we issued actually improve the customer's reported satisfaction?
  • Which response channels (email, phone, portal) have the highest response rates?
  • What is the relationship between late delivery and satisfaction score in our data?

Core Concept

Structuring Feedback as Data

The resp_text field preserves the customer's words. The resp_sentiment field makes those words queryable. The two fields serve different purposes and should both be kept: free text captures nuance that classification flattens; structured classification enables the aggregation that free text prevents.

resp_score — operational satisfaction; measured per transaction
resp_nps — relationship loyalty; measures likelihood to recommend; typically collected less frequently
resp_sentiment — qualitative signal; derived from text or manual review; bridges narrative to analytics

A response table that captures all three gives you three distinct lenses: how did this specific delivery go (score), how loyal is this customer overall (NPS), and what tone are they using when they talk about us (sentiment).

Core Concept

NPS: Promoters, Passives, Detractors

NPS (Net Promoter Score) classifies customers by their resp_nps value into three groups. The score itself is calculated as: % Promoters − % Detractors. It ranges from −100 to +100. The classification is what makes it actionable by segment.

Promoters (9–10) — loyal advocates; likely to refer new customers
Passives (7–8) — satisfied but not enthusiastic; vulnerable to competitive offers
Detractors (0–6) — unhappy; at risk of churn and negative word-of-mouth

NPS is most valuable when trended over time and segmented by customer type or delivery performance — not as a single aggregate number.
Core Concept

The Follow-Up Queue

The resp_followup_required flag transforms the response table from a historical record into an active queue — similar to how adj_status = 'Pending' creates the adjustment queue in Chapter 7. When a response below a defined score threshold is received, resp_followup_required is set to TRUE. That flag, combined with a NULL resp_followup_date, identifies every open follow-up: responses that need action but have not yet received it.

resp_followup_required = TRUE, resp_followup_date IS NULL — open; action needed
resp_followup_required = TRUE, resp_followup_date IS NOT NULL, resp_resolved = TRUE — closed successfully
resp_followup_required = TRUE, resp_followup_date IS NOT NULL, resp_resolved = FALSE — closed but unresolved; escalation candidate

Organizations that consistently close their follow-up queue have measurably higher retention rates than those that treat negative feedback as a log entry.

Analytical Application

Queries That Become Possible

Satisfaction score by delivery performance band

SELECT
  CASE
    WHEN d.del_date < o.ord_promised_date THEN 'Early'
    WHEN d.del_date = o.ord_promised_date THEN 'On Time'
    WHEN d.del_date <= o.ord_promised_date + INTERVAL '3 days' THEN 'Late ≤3d'
    ELSE 'Late >3d'
  END AS delivery_band,
  COUNT(r.resp_id) AS response_count,
  ROUND(AVG(r.resp_score), 2) AS avg_satisfaction,
  ROUND(AVG(r.resp_nps), 2) AS avg_nps
FROM response r
JOIN delivery d ON r.del_id = d.del_id
JOIN "order" o ON d.ord_id = o.ord_id
WHERE r.resp_score IS NOT NULL AND o.ord_promised_date IS NOT NULL
GROUP BY delivery_band
ORDER BY avg_satisfaction DESC;

This query quantifies the satisfaction cost of lateness — making the business case for on-time delivery in the customer's own words.

NPS by customer segment and quarter

SELECT
  DATE_TRUNC('quarter', r.resp_date) AS quarter,
  c.cust_type,
  COUNT(r.resp_id) AS responses,
  ROUND(
    100.0 * COUNT(CASE WHEN r.resp_nps >= 9 THEN 1 END) / NULLIF(COUNT(r.resp_nps), 0)
    - 100.0 * COUNT(CASE WHEN r.resp_nps <= 6 THEN 1 END) / NULLIF(COUNT(r.resp_nps), 0)
  , 1) AS nps_score
FROM response r
JOIN customer c ON r.cust_id = c.cust_id
WHERE r.resp_nps IS NOT NULL
GROUP BY DATE_TRUNC('quarter', r.resp_date), c.cust_type
ORDER BY quarter ASC, nps_score DESC;

Trending NPS by quarter reveals whether relationship loyalty is improving or eroding — segmented by the customer types that matter most to your business.

Adjustment impact on satisfaction: did the correction work?

SELECT
  c.cust_name, o.ord_id,
  COUNT(a.adj_id) AS adjustments_on_order,
  ROUND(SUM(ABS(a.adj_price)), 2) AS total_adj_value,
  r.resp_score,
  r.resp_nps,
  r.resp_sentiment,
  r.resp_resolved
FROM response r
JOIN delivery d ON r.del_id = d.del_id
JOIN "order" o ON d.ord_id = o.ord_id
JOIN customer c ON r.cust_id = c.cust_id
LEFT JOIN adjustment a ON a.ord_id = o.ord_id AND a.adj_status = 'Applied'
WHERE a.adj_id IS NOT NULL
GROUP BY c.cust_name, o.ord_id, r.resp_score, r.resp_nps, r.resp_sentiment, r.resp_resolved
ORDER BY adjustments_on_order DESC, r.resp_score ASC;

Orders with high adjustment values but strong satisfaction scores indicate the correction process is working. Low scores despite corrections signal a deeper relationship issue.

Open follow-up queue — negative responses awaiting action

SELECT
  r.resp_id, c.cust_name, c.cust_type,
  r.resp_date, r.resp_score, r.resp_nps,
  r.resp_sentiment, r.resp_text,
  CURRENT_DATE - r.resp_date AS days_open
FROM response r
JOIN customer c ON r.cust_id = c.cust_id
WHERE r.resp_followup_required = TRUE
  AND r.resp_followup_date IS NULL
ORDER BY r.resp_score ASC, r.resp_date ASC;

Sort by score first so the most critical voices are always at the top of the queue. days_open surfaces how long customers have been waiting to hear back.

Silent customers — no response in the last 90 days

SELECT
  c.cust_id, c.cust_name, c.cust_type,
  MAX(r.resp_date) AS last_response_date,
  CURRENT_DATE - MAX(r.resp_date) AS days_since_last_response,
  COUNT(o.ord_id) AS orders_in_window
FROM customer c
JOIN "order" o ON c.cust_id = o.cust_id
  AND o.ord_exp_date >= CURRENT_DATE - INTERVAL '90 days'
LEFT JOIN response r ON c.cust_id = r.cust_id
GROUP BY c.cust_id, c.cust_name, c.cust_type
HAVING MAX(r.resp_date) < CURRENT_DATE - INTERVAL '90 days'
  OR MAX(r.resp_date) IS NULL
ORDER BY days_since_last_response DESC;

Silent customers are not necessarily satisfied — they may have disengaged. Orders in window confirms they have been active recently, making the silence more significant.

Reflection

Questions Worth Sitting With

  • Think about the last time a customer expressed dissatisfaction. Was that feedback connected to a specific delivery, order, or adjustment in your data — or did it exist only in an email thread, a CRM note, or someone's memory?
  • Consider the distinction between a customer who complains and a customer who goes silent. Which is more dangerous to your business? Which is more visible in your current data?
  • If you ran the follow-up queue query against your current data, how many open negative responses would surface? What would the average days_open be? Is there a threshold your organization has agreed on — and is it being met?
  • Think about your highest-value customers. When did you last receive a response from each of them? Could any of them be silent right now, with active orders, and no feedback in the system?
  • The adjustment-to-satisfaction query asks whether your corrections are actually working. Think of a recent case where a significant credit or adjustment was issued. Did you measure whether the customer's satisfaction improved afterward — or was issuing the credit considered the end of the process?
